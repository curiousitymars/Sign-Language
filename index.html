Introduction to Sign Language

In this website, you will learn the basics of Sign Language, and gain the capability to converse. Click on the sections to learn each part.

Alphabet

The sign language alphabet takes practice to master, but is one of the most important components of sign language. On meeting a deaf person for the first time, you should spell out your name using the alphabet. This can also be useful for unknown signs or gestures.

A

Letter A

The letter A is signed by holding up your dominant hand in a fist, facing outward, with the thumb sticking up to the side of the fist. This looks like the small letter 'a', but upside down, where the thumb sticking up reminds you of the serif sticking out the side of the small letter 'a'.

B

Letter B

The letter B is signed by holding up your dominant hand open, with palm facing out and all 4 fingers standing straight and kept together, while tucking your thumb into your palm. This somehow resembles the small letter 'b'.

C

Letter C

The letter C is signed by curving your open, dominant hand just like the letter 'C', where the top 4 fingers held together make up the top curve, and the thumb makes up the bottom curve.

D

Letter D

The letter D is signed by holding up your dominant hand, curving your middle, ring, and pinkie fingers together and touching them to your thumb, while only your index finger is standing upright. This looks just like the small letter 'd'.

E

Letter E

The letter E is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with your top 4 fingers all touching each other and tightly curved in, while your thumb is also curved and tightly tucked into your palm, touching the tips of the fingers above. This looks just like the small letter 'e'.

F

Letter F

The letter F is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with your index finger and thumb touching, while your 3 other fingers are held up and spread apart. Make sure to form a circle with your thumb and index finger touching.

G

Letter G

The letter G is signed by holding out your dominant hand in a fist, with knuckles lined up vertically. Keep your middle, ring, and pinkie fingers curled in, while your index finger and thumb stick out parallel to each other.

H

Letter H

The letter H is signed by holding out your dominant hand horizontally, palm facing in, with your index and middle fingers held out together, straight and stacked horizontally, index at the top. The rest of the fingers and thumb are curled in.

I

Letter I

The letter I is signed by holding up your dominant hand in a fist, palm facing out, with only your pinkie finger sticking up straight.

J

Letter J

The letter J is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with your fingers curled into a fist except your pinkie sticking out. Then make a 'J' in the air with your pinkie finger, starting from the top of the 'J' stem down and curving up again to form the bottom part of the letter.

K

Letter K

The letter K is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with your index and middle fingers sticking up like the letter 'V' (or like the common "peace" sign), while your thumb is tucked at the base of the two extended fingers. Your two remaining fingers are curled in, touching your palm.

L

Letter L

The letter L is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with your thumb and index finger sticking out at a right angle to each other, while the last 3 fingers are curled in. This is the capital letter 'L'.

M

Letter M

The letter M is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with all your fingers curled into your palm. Then tuck your thumb between your ring and pinkie fingers. This sign looks like the small letter 'm', with the 3 fingers' bumps reminding us of the letter written in cursive.

N

Letter N

The letter N is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with all your fingers curled into your palm. Then tuck your thumb between your middle and ring fingers. This sign looks like the small letter 'n', with the 2 fingers' bumps reminding us of the letter written in cursive.

O

Letter O

The letter O is signed by curving all your fingers and touching them to your thumb, making the shape of the letter 'O' with your entire hand.

P

Letter P

The letter P is signed like K, but the former is held upside down. Hold down your dominant hand, palm facing in, with your index and middle fingers sticking out like an inverted letter 'V', while your thumb is tucked in between the two extended fingers.

Q

Letter Q

The letter Q is signed by holding down your dominant hand, palm facing in, with your with knuckles lined up horizontally. Keep your middle, ring, and pinkie fingers curled in, while your index finger and thumb stick out parallel to each other, like two legs of a person standing up.

R

Letter R

The letter R is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with your index and middle fingers intertwined like vines. The rest of your fingers and thumb are curled in. This looks just like the small letter 'r'.

S

Letter S

The letter S is signed by holding up your dominant hand into a fist, palm facing out. Make sure your thumb is tucked in front of your fingers. Otherwise, you might sign the letter A by mistake.

T

Letter T

The letter T is signed by holding up your dominant hand into a fist, palm facing out, and your thumb tucked in between your index and middle fingers.

U

Letter U

The letter U is signed by holding up your dominant hand in a fist, facing outward, with the index & middle fingers sticking up while held together.

V

Letter V

The letter V is signed by holding up your dominant hand in a fist, facing outward, with the index & middle fingers sticking out and spread apart. It looks just like the letter 'V'.

W

Letter W

The letter W is signed by holding up your dominant hand in a fist, palm facing out, with your index, middle, and ring fingers sticking out. This looks just like the letter 'W'. Be careful not to confuse this with the number '3' in ASL, where your thumb sticks out instead of your ring finger.

X

Letter X

The letter X is signed by holding up your dominant hand in a fist, palm facing out. Then stick out your index finger and bend it into a hook.

Y

Letter Y

The letter Y is signed by holding up your dominant hand, palm facing out, with your thumb and pinkie finger sticking out and the rest of your fingers curled in.

Z

Letter Z

The letter Z is signed by holding up the index finger of your dominant hand, palm facing out with the rest of the fingers drawn into a fist, and tracing the letter 'Z' with your index in the air.

Sight Words

Basic sight words of sign language build up the language and have consistent usage. They are simple gestures but must be articulated correctly to avoid improper meaning. Unknown words however can still be spelled out using the ASL Alphabet. Here you will find the most commonly used sign language sight words. And remember that in sign language a noun shall be signed two times while a verb shall only be signed once.

Done

Done

To sign "done", start with your palms inwards andv turn them to face them out. Keep doing this to sign the word.

More

More

To sign "more", bring your fingers and thumb together on each hand, making it look like an 'O' which has been laid flat. Pull your hands away from each other and back again.

Help

Help

To sign "help", put a closed fist with a thumbs up on a flat palm, using both hands. Then, simply proceed to to move both hands upward.

Eat

Eat

To sign "eat", close your fingers, making a kind of flat 'o' and push your hand to your mouth. Do this only once, as if you sign this twice that will mean "food" instead of "eat". This can be used as a nonverbal way of conveying that you are hungry.

Please

Please

To sign "please", hold your fingers together with the thumb sticking outwards and circle it below your shoulder.

Hungry

Hungry

To sign "hungry", make a 'c' shape towards yourself and move it down.

Thank You

Thank You

To sign "thank you", put your fingers on your chin and extend it outwards.

Happy

Happy

To sign "happy", take open palms and brush up below your shoulder.

Want

Want

To sign "want" place your hands out, with hands open and palms facing upward, thus making your hands bent a little into a claw shape. Lastly, pull both your hands toward you.

Good Morning

Good Morning

To sign "good morning", put one hand on your chin and move it down to touch the other. Then, move the first hand under the second and place the second hand in the middle of it.

Good Night

Good Night

To sign "good night", put one hand on your chin and move it down to touch the other. Then, move the first hand on top of the second.

Family

One important topic of sign language is knowing how to adress specific members of the family.

Grandfather

To sign this, stretch out all fingers on your hand, and put your thumb on your head, only to move it in an arc shape twice.

Grandmother

To sign "grandmother", stretch out all fingers on your hand, and put your thumb on your chin, only to move it in an arc shape twice, similar to "grandfather".

Father

"Father" is signed very similar to grandfather, stretch out all fingers on your hand, and put your thumb on your forhead, then move it in an arc shape once only.

Mother

Similar to "grandmother", "mother" is signed by stretching out all fingers on your hand, and putting your thumb on your chin, and then moving it in an arc shape once only.

Aunt

In order to sign "aunt", make a thumbs-up and circle it towards your cheek.

Uncle

This is signed by placing two fingers next to your forhead and moving it in small circles.

Uncle

Make a flat 'l' shape with one hand and curve your other fingers in. Keep that hand next to your forhead, and close to your stomach, make the letter "g" in sign language with your thumb sticking slightly upwards. Then move the top hand to the bottom one.

Sister

Make a flat 'l' shape with one hand and curve your other fingers in. Keep that hand next to your chin, and close to your stomach, make the letter "g" in sign language with your thumb sticking slightly upwards. Then finally move the top hand to the bottom one.

Cousin

Make a "c" shape with your hand and place it next to your cheek. Then, simply twist it back and forth.

Descriptions

In conversations, describing things is one of the most important aspects. The same is true for Sign Language. Colors, sizes, lengths, and more are are all important.

Red

Put a finger on your chin and slide it down.

Green

Under your shoulder, make a flat "l" in sign language and curve your other fingers in. Then, simply twist it back and forth.

Orange

Place your hand under your chin and squeeze your fingers into a fist and loosen them once more a few times.

Blue

Put all of your fingers together under your shoulder with the thumb slightly tucked behind the first finger then, simply twist it back and forth.

Yellow

Hold up the letter 'y' in sign language and twist your hand back and forth.

Black

Make a flat 'l' and curve your other fingers in. Keep the thumb slightly sticking out from the back. Then, simply drag the hand back and forth across your forhead.

Brown

Make an open palm and drag it up and down your cheek.

White

Place an open hand below your neck and close your fist.

Purple

Make letter 'p' in ASL and shake it around yourself.

Purple

Make a slight 'l' shape curving your first finger slightly with both hands and extend them outwards.

Small

Extend youur palms on opposite sides and push them close together.

Long

Stretch out one hand and put a finger on top of it. Then, raise the fignger to your shoulder.

Short

Hold up an upside down palm and push it down.

Grammar and Sentence Formation

Grammar is one of the most important parts of sentence structure. In Sign Language, there are signs for every plural verb, singular verb, etc.

Am

Make letter 'a' and put the thumb on your chin. Then, more your hand slightly forward.

Are

Cross your fingers to make letter 'r' and place it on your mouth. Then, move it away.

Is

Simply fingerspell the letters 'i' and 's'.

Go

Point both fingers up towards your face and then move them in front of you.

Think

Point your finger upwards and then point it towards your head until you touch it.

Do

Hold up two hands with your thumb and first finger close together and your other fingers curved in.

What

Hold up your hands with your forward tilted wrists and move it up. Then turn your palms over.

How

Make the letter 's' in sign language and face it towards yourself. Then, move it upwards and spread your fingers outward.

When

Put one hand above another and on both hands, put out your index finger. Then, make a circular motion in front of you with the higher hand and touch your lower index finger.

Now to form sentences, you can simply combine the verbs with the vocabulary and descriptions.
